Carole Cadwalladr:
The Rise of the Broligarchy

Summary
Investigative journalist Carole Cadwalladr — winner of the Orwell Prize and known for her pivotal investigation into Cambridge Analytica and the Brexit campaign — delivers one of TED 2025's most provocative and urgent talks. The talk centres on what she terms the "broligarchy": a small group of immensely powerful male tech executives who leverage their control of global digital platforms — social media, communication infrastructure, AI systems — to accumulate unprecedented geopolitical power while dismantling democratic institutions and enabling authoritarian governments worldwide. Cadwalladr argues that the international order built after World War II is being systematically dismantled in real time through data harvesting, algorithmic manipulation, and the privatisation of public discourse. She calls for mass digital disobedience, urging individuals to resist surveillance, support independent journalism, and understand that every data point surrendered to tech platforms is a transfer of power. Her rallying cry — "you have more power than you think" — challenges the helplessness many citizens feel in the face of tech monopolies.
💬
My Opinion
Cadwalladr's talk is not comfortable viewing, and I believe that discomfort is entirely intentional. As someone from Sri Lanka — a country where political polarisation, social media manipulation, and the erosion of public trust in institutions are lived realities — her warnings feel particularly resonant. The concept of the broligarchy is a coined term but a precise one: it captures the specific intersection of tech power, gender dynamics, and political influence in a way that generic terms like "big tech" do not. What I found most remarkable, communicatively, was Cadwalladr's controlled fury — she spoke with the calm, measured precision of someone who has marshalled overwhelming evidence but is deliberately restraining the emotional weight of it, which made the talk all the more devastating. This is a powerful rhetorical technique: restraint in the face of outrage often communicates more intensity than open anger.
